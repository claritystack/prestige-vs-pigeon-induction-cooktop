Prestige vs Pigeon Induction Cooktop: Head-to-Head Comparison

1. Wattage and Heating Performance

The Pigeon Cruise runs at 1800W — 200W more than the Prestige PIC 20's 1600W. On paper this means the Pigeon heats faster, boils water sooner, and reaches high temperatures more quickly. For buyers who want maximum raw heating power for the money, the Pigeon wins this number. In real-world kitchen use, the difference between 1600W and 1800W is roughly 30 to 45 seconds on a full pot of water — noticeable in a side-by-side test, largely irrelevant during actual cooking where the bottleneck is never how fast the water initially heats.

The more meaningful heating difference is in how each cooktop manages power once the cooking temperature is reached. The Prestige PIC 20 uses sensor-based intelligent power control that continuously modulates wattage in real time to maintain target temperature — rather than cycling between full power and zero power the way simpler induction cooktops work. This prevents the overshooting and temperature dipping that causes milk to boil over suddenly or dal to stick at the bottom despite being on a low setting. It also uses vessel-sensing technology that detects the size of the vessel placed on the glass and adjusts the power output accordingly — a large pressure cooker gets full power, a small milk pan gets appropriately reduced power without manual adjustment.

The Pigeon Cruise does not specify intelligent power modulation — it operates at its set power level without sensor-based adjustment. For simple high-heat cooking tasks this is entirely adequate. For precise temperature-dependent cooking like making dosas at consistent medium-low heat or pressure cooking that needs sustained pressure without overshooting, the Prestige's power intelligence is a daily practical advantage.

Pigeon wins on raw wattage at 1800W vs 1600W. Prestige wins on intelligent power management — sensor-based temperature modulation and vessel-size detection make it the more capable cooktop for varied Indian cooking beyond basic boiling and frying.

2. Preset Menus and Indian Cooking

This is where the Prestige PIC 20 justifies most of its premium over the Pigeon Cruise, and it is worth understanding what Indian preset menus on an induction cooktop actually do versus what they sound like they do. Each preset is not simply a named button that runs the cooktop at a fixed temperature — it runs a pre-programmed combination of temperature range, power modulation pattern, and timing that is calibrated for the specific thermal behaviour of that dish. Pressure Cook runs sustained high heat to build and maintain pressure. Dosa runs medium-low sustained heat with slight variations to replicate the behaviour of a cast iron tawa being used on a gas flame. Idli runs a gentle steaming-level heat. Keep Warm runs at the minimum temperature needed to hold food without cooking it further. Deep Fry runs at the sustained high temperature needed for oil to stay at frying temperature as food is added and lowers it momentarily.

For a household that cooks all of these dishes regularly — which describes the majority of Indian families — having calibrated presets means consistently better results with less monitoring. You do not need to stand over the cooktop adjusting heat levels while simultaneously managing other parts of the meal. The preset does the management for you. This is the same reason Indian menu presets are a premium feature on microwave ovens — the convenience of calibrated automation for dishes you cook five times a week compounds into meaningful time and effort savings.

The Pigeon Cruise does not specify preset Indian menus. It offers manual temperature and power control — which works perfectly well for experienced cooks who know their temperature settings by habit. For households where multiple family members use the cooktop with varying cooking experience, the Prestige's presets mean anyone in the house can cook a consistent dosa or pressure cook dal without needing to know what temperature setting to use.

Prestige wins decisively on Indian cooking intelligence — 8 calibrated preset menus covering the full range of daily Indian cooking versus Pigeon's manual-only control. For households cooking varied Indian meals daily, this is the most practically impactful difference between the two cooktops.

3. Voltage Protection and Safety

The Prestige PIC 20's 4KV surge protection is the specification that matters most to Indian buyers in cities and towns where voltage stability is not guaranteed. A 4KV rating means the cooktop's internal electronics are protected against voltage spikes of up to 4,000 volts — the kind that occur during lightning strikes near the power grid, during sudden load shedding restoration, or during the voltage fluctuations that are an everyday occurrence across large parts of India outside metro areas. Without surge protection, a single significant voltage spike can permanently damage the induction coil or the control circuitry, turning a ₹3,000 appliance into scrap without any warning.

The Automatic Voltage Regulator on the Prestige handles the more common problem of sustained low or high voltage — not spikes but steady fluctuation. Indian household voltage commonly drops to 180-200V during peak demand periods and rises above 240V at low-demand times. AVR cushions these variations and maintains stable power delivery to the cooktop's electronics, extending the lifespan of the appliance in real Indian grid conditions. This is not theoretical protection — it is why Prestige appliances consistently show longer lifespans in buyer reviews from tier-2 and tier-3 cities where grid quality is most variable.

The Pigeon Cruise specifies short circuit protection — protection against the appliance's own internal wiring failure — but does not specify external surge protection or voltage regulation. For buyers in cities with stable power supply, this difference matters less. For buyers in areas with frequent voltage fluctuations, power cuts, or monsoon-season electrical instability, the Prestige's protection specifications are a meaningful long-term durability advantage that partially justifies the price premium on their own.

Prestige wins significantly on electrical protection — 4KV surge protection and Automatic Voltage Regulation versus Pigeon's basic short circuit protection only. For buyers in areas with unstable power supply, this difference alone justifies a significant portion of the ₹951 premium.

What to Look for in an Induction Cooktop in India

Wattage determines heat speed, not cooking quality. Higher wattage means faster initial heating and boiling. 1600W to 1800W is the standard range for Indian household cooktops and is sufficient for all daily cooking tasks including pressure cooking and deep frying. Above 2000W is available on larger models but requires a dedicated circuit in many older Indian homes. Below 1400W is insufficient for efficient pressure cooking. Both the Prestige and Pigeon fall within the ideal range for Indian household use.

Indian preset menus are more useful than they appear on a spec sheet. Every Indian kitchen cooks the same core set of dishes repeatedly — dal, rice, curry, chai, dosas. Presets calibrated for these dishes deliver better results with less monitoring than manual temperature control, particularly for cooks who are new to induction cooking and have not yet learned the temperature equivalents of their gas flame intuition. If you cook a variety of Indian dishes daily, presets pay for themselves in reduced attention and better consistency within a month of use.

Surge protection matters more in India than anywhere else. Indian household voltage is nominally 220-230V but fluctuates significantly in practice, particularly in smaller cities, rural areas, and during monsoon season. A cooktop without surge protection is vulnerable to voltage spike damage that voids the warranty and requires full replacement. If you live in an area with any history of voltage instability, surge protection is not optional — it is cheap insurance on a daily-use appliance.

Induction cookware compatibility is the hidden cost most buyers forget. Induction cooktops only work with cookware that has a magnetic base — cast iron, magnetic stainless steel, and most modern hard-anodised aluminium with induction bases. Standard aluminium pressure cookers, copper-bottom vessels, and old stainless steel cookware without magnetic bases will not work. Before buying an induction cooktop, check your existing cookware with a magnet — if the magnet sticks to the base, the vessel is induction compatible. Budget ₹500 to ₹1,500 for a basic induction-compatible pressure cooker if your existing one is not compatible.

Crystal glass surface is standard at this price — handle it accordingly. Both cooktops use a crystal glass top plate that is smooth, easy to clean, and highly durable under normal use. It will crack if a heavy vessel is dropped on it from height or if cold water is poured on a hot surface. Standard precautions apply — do not slide heavy cast iron across it, let it cool before cleaning, and do not use abrasive pads on the surface. Under normal cooking conditions, crystal glass induction tops last the full warranty period and well beyond without issues.

Can I use a pressure cooker on an induction cooktop?

Yes — but only if the pressure cooker has a magnetic base. Most modern induction-compatible pressure cookers are made from magnetic stainless steel and are specifically labelled as induction-compatible. Standard aluminium pressure cookers — which are the most common type in Indian kitchens — are not induction compatible. Test your existing pressure cooker by holding a magnet to the base: if it sticks firmly, it is induction compatible. If it does not stick, you will need to buy an induction-compatible pressure cooker before using these cooktops for dal or rice. Budget ₹600 to ₹1,200 for a basic induction-compatible model.

3)

Is induction cooking safe for Indian kitchens?

Yes — induction cooking is significantly safer than gas cooking for Indian kitchens. There is no open flame, no gas leak risk, and no combustion. The cooktop surface only heats where an induction-compatible vessel is placed — the rest of the glass surface stays cool to the touch even while cooking, dramatically reducing accidental burn risk particularly in households with children. The vessel detection feature means the cooktop will not activate without a compatible vessel on the surface, eliminating the risk of an accidentally switched-on element burning a hand. Both the Prestige PIC 20 and Pigeon Cruise include auto switch-off that cuts power when the timer expires or the vessel is removed.

4)

Does an induction cooktop consume more electricity than a gas stove?

No — induction cooktops are significantly more energy efficient than gas stoves. A gas stove converts roughly 40% of its energy into useful cooking heat, with the rest lost to the surrounding air and kitchen environment. An induction cooktop converts approximately 85 to 90% of its electrical energy directly into heat in the vessel. In practice this means an induction cooktop cooks the same meal using significantly less energy than gas. The efficiency advantage becomes more meaningful as LPG cylinder prices rise — many households that switched from gas to induction as a primary cooktop in the last three years report lower total monthly cooking energy costs even after accounting for electricity tariffs.

5)

What is the difference between 1600W and 1800W induction cooktop?

The practical difference is heating speed — an 1800W cooktop will boil a litre of water approximately 30 to 45 seconds faster than a 1600W cooktop. In real cooking conditions, this difference is minor and rarely the deciding factor in a purchase decision. What matters more for cooking quality is how the cooktop manages power once the target temperature is reached — whether it maintains consistent heat through intelligent modulation or simply cycles between full power and zero power. The Prestige PIC 20 at 1600W with intelligent power management delivers better cooking results for temperature-sensitive Indian dishes than a basic 1800W cooktop that lacks comparable power control.

6)

How long do induction cooktops last?

A well-maintained induction cooktop from a quality brand typically lasts 5 to 8 years with daily use. The main failure modes are the induction coil wearing out after extended high-power use, the control electronics failing due to voltage spikes or heat damage, and the glass surface cracking from impact or thermal shock. Surge protection as on the Prestige PIC 20 directly addresses the most common electronics failure mode in the Indian context. To extend lifespan: do not pour cold water on a hot surface, do not slide heavy cast iron pots across the glass, run the cooktop in a ventilated area so the cooling fan is not obstructed, and wipe the surface clean after each use to prevent grease buildup around the edges.
